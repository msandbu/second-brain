The Scale of AI and Infrastructure Growth Wrapping up the year 2025, the podcast reflects on the explosive three-year journey since the launch of ChatGPT, which now boasts 800 million unique monthly users and a massive ecosystem of 4 million developers
. This rapid growth has triggered roughly 5 trillion NOK in public investments aimed at building data centers and securing hardware
. The immense demand for computing power, particularly Nvidia GPUs, has disrupted consumer markets—making RAM more expensive and forcing Nvidia to scale back on gaming graphics cards to prioritize AI
.
To combat hardware bottlenecks and high costs, companies are diversifying their infrastructure. Google has developed highly efficient AI chips called TPUs (Tensor Processing Units), with their latest v7 rivaling Nvidia's performance while consuming less power and costing less to produce
. Consequently, major AI developers like Anthropic are actively avoiding vendor lock-in by utilizing a mix of Google TPUs, Nvidia GPUs, and AWS chips (like Trainium) to power their platforms
.
Environmental Impact of AI Agents A major emerging topic is the environmental cost of AI compute
. While a standard text query on Google Gemini uses only about five drops of water for cooling, the landscape is changing as we move toward AI agents
. When an AI agent is tasked with a multi-step process—such as taking control of a browser to do Christmas grocery shopping on Oda—it requires 10 to 20 times more processing power, increasing the water cost to roughly 100 drops per task
.
From General to Specialized and Local Models AI has evolved dramatically from "magical" text generation three years ago to generating realistic video and executing complex PC tasks today
. The market is now being flooded with new models (like Gemini 3 Pro, GPT-5.2, and Claude 4.5 Opus), but the most notable shift is toward smaller, open-source, and highly specialized models
.
Instead of relying solely on massive, generic models that try to be good at everything (from poetry analysis to coding), companies are building purpose-driven AI
. For example, Microsoft has developed a model specifically trained to control computers
. Furthermore, AI is moving locally; Microsoft's Copilot+ PCs now feature dedicated AI chips that run local language models directly on the device
. Upcoming models are also expected to feature continuous self-training capabilities and integrated memory to adapt to user preferences over time
.
AI Adoption in Norway and the SME Challenge While Norway ranks relatively high in some AI adoption reports, the country faces unique structural challenges
. Since 99% of Norwegian businesses are Small and Medium Enterprises (SMEs) with fewer than 200 employees, most lack the massive IT departments needed to seamlessly integrate AI into complex legacy systems like Visma or Gatt
.
The host stresses that Norway should not attempt to compete with global tech giants by building massive foundational language models from scratch
. Instead, the strategy should be to take powerful existing models, fine-tune them with localized Norwegian data, and build innovative applications and agents on top of them
.
Security and the "Year of the Agent" Looking ahead to 2026 and 2027, the tech industry is heavily betting on autonomous agents, a focus strongly highlighted at recent Amazon and Microsoft conferences
. As these agents take on more responsibilities—communicating with business software and executing automated workflows—security and data sovereignty will become critical concerns
. Relying entirely on foreign tech giants means Norwegian businesses must be highly vigilant about how their data flows between services, and how AI interacts with their internal systems
.
