AI in the Municipal Sector: Productivity and Case Management
Introduction and Personal AI Integration
Artificial intelligence is increasingly becoming integrated into daily workflows, functioning almost as "co-intelligence"
. However, to use these tools effectively, it is essential to maintain a critical mindset, treating AI outputs with the same skepticism one might have for a door-to-door salesperson
. In both personal and professional settings, language models serve as dialogue partners to improve the quality of ideas, evaluate risks, and brainstorm solutions
. AI is also highly effective for practical tasks, such as generating drafts for meeting minutes—which should always be reviewed manually—and troubleshooting everyday issues by analyzing photos of malfunctioning equipment or error codes
.
Challenges in the Municipal Sector
Implementing AI in the municipal sector faces several significant hurdles, notably related to time, legal compliance, and economics
. Municipalities must navigate complex legal landscapes, with AI initiatives touching upon up to 14 different legal frameworks, including the GDPR and the upcoming AI Act, which carries massive potential fines for non-compliance
. Furthermore, with 70% of municipalities currently running a deficit, acquiring expensive commercial AI licenses represents a financial risk that requires careful consideration
. Another major challenge is benefit realization; while AI may increase individual productivity, this does not automatically translate to direct cost savings for a municipality unless it directly affects long-term hiring needs and capacity
.
AI in Case Management and Open Innovation
Despite these structural challenges, municipal projects are actively exploring AI integration into core tasks like public case management
. Because automated decision-making currently faces strict limitations under privacy laws, implementations must be carefully designed to maintain public trust
. To tackle this, a collaborative approach involving multiple municipalities and a joint IT company has been launched following a nearly year-long procurement process
. Emphasizing the Norwegian tradition of "dugnad" (community cooperation), the project prioritizes open innovation
. The insights, best practices, and developed use cases are shared openly on a dedicated website to help other municipalities scale their efforts and avoid common regulatory and technical pitfalls
.
Identifying Use Cases and Prototyping
When prioritizing which AI use cases to tackle first, municipalities evaluate the time currently spent on the manual process, the availability of existing market solutions, and the legal complexity involved
. The primary goal remains maximizing efficiency and cost savings to counter the pressures of an aging population and tight municipal budgets
. To accelerate development and procurement, rapid prototyping tools—often referred to as "vibe coding"—are utilized
. By using AI-driven code generators, teams can create functional application prototypes and websites in mere minutes, drastically improving communication with developers and reducing the time spent drafting traditional requirement specifications
.
The Future of AI and Handling Errors
The landscape of artificial intelligence is evolving at an unprecedented pace
. Capabilities that would have required billions of dollars and thousands of manual workers just a few years ago are now widely accessible and highly affordable for local governments
. While AI hallucinations remain a valid concern for public sector implementation, continuous model updates are steadily reducing error rates
. Ultimately, as AI reliability improves, it is acknowledged that human workers also make regular errors, making the cautious and well-regulated integration of AI both a practical and necessary step forward for the public sector
.
